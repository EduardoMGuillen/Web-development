The Criminal Defense Process

Understanding your rights and the legal process when facing criminal charges

1

Arrest & Charges Filed

Law enforcement arrests you or issues a summons. The prosecutor reviews the evidence and decides what charges to file. In some cases, you may be released on bond or remain in custody until your first hearing.

Immediate
2

Initial Court Appearance / Arraignment

You're formally told of the charges against you. The judge advises you of your rights and sets or reviews bond. You enter a plea (usually "not guilty" at this stage).

1-2 weeks
3

Investigation & Evidence Gathering

Your defense attorney requests "discovery" (the evidence the State plans to use). This may include police reports, videos, witness statements, and lab results. Your attorney also begins an independent investigation—collecting documents, interviewing witnesses, and reviewing the State's case for weaknesses.

2-8 weeks
4

Pre-Trial Motions & Negotiations

Your attorney may file motions (to suppress evidence, dismiss charges, etc.). The defense and prosecution may negotiate for a plea agreement. The judge may hold pre-trial hearings to resolve disputes before trial.

2-6 weeks
5

Trial (If No Plea Agreement Reached)

A jury or judge hears the case. Both sides present evidence, question witnesses, and make arguments. The jury or judge decides whether the State has proven guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.

1-4 weeks
6

Sentencing & Possible Appeals

If found guilty (by plea or trial), the court imposes a sentence (probation, fines, jail, or prison). If not guilty, you are acquitted, and the case is dismissed. If convicted, you may pursue an appeal or post-conviction relief.

Varies
